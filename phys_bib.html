Annotated physics bibliography

A collection of reviews of physics texts, based on my experience as an undergrad in applied physics at Cornell. The intent is to point out what makes each book unique; why it is better or worse than other books at the same level. Organized by topic. Within a topic, difficulty increases as you go down.

Classical mechanics

Kleppner & Kolenkow - An Introduction to Mechanics

Used in Phys 1116.

Taylor - Classical Mechanics

Used sometimes in AEP 3330 and Phys 3318.

Landau & Lifshitz - Mechanics

The following hold for all books in the Course of Theoretical Physics: Derivations often follow from physical and symmetry principles, or simply from intelligent mathematical fiddling. Expects a level of physical and mathematical intuition out of the reader; otherwise it's hard to follow along. Beautifully written.

Mechanics covers everything you would expect in an undergrad class; there are no real gaps, which is surprising considering how short it is. All of its mathematical machinery is applied very elegantly, so derivations just happen in the most natural way possible. This is the book's greatest strength and its greatest weakness: It feels like experiencing the way a fantastic theorist thinks, but that doesn't necessarily prepare you to attack problems on your own.

Goldstein, Poole, & Safko - Classical Mechanics

Classical electromagnetism

Purcell & Morin - Electricity and Magnetism

Used in Phys 2217.

Griffiths - Introduction to Electrodynamics

Used in AEP 3560.

Staelin, Morgenthaler, & Kong - Electromagnetic Waves

Used in AEP 3560. Higher level than Griffiths as far as the math goes.

Heald & Marion - Classical Electromagnetic Radiation

Used in Phys 3327. Just as Marion's mechanics book tries to be Goldstein-lite, this one tries to be Jackson-lite. It touches on more topics, with more depth, than Griffihs. Unfortunately, its mathematical treatment is belabored, and often involves confusing manipulations of delta functions. If you have the math, you can skip straight from Griffiths to Landau and Jackson.

Landau & Lifshitz - The Classical Theory of Fields

Probably the most beautiful physics book ever written. Begins with an illuminating discussion of special relativity and relativistic mechanics. Like a true theorist, Landau builds E&M out of the four-potential, guage invariance, and Lorentz transformations. His treatment of radiation is the best of any book, ever. Everything here is about E&M in free space; if you want to deal with it in media, look to the next book.

Landau, Lifshitz, & Pitaevskii - Electrodynamics of Continuous Media

Jackson - Classical Electrodynamics

Used in Phys 6561 and pretty much every graduate E&M class in the country. That's because there's no better treatment of E&M out there (besides Landau).

Statistical mechanics

Goodstein - States of Matter

This book is cool because it teaches by example. The first chapter is on the fundamentals of stat mech, but then each of the following chapters covers a specific area: Gases, solids, liquids, exotic phases, and phase transitions.

Hill - Statistical Mechanics: Principles and & Selected Applications

A straightforward, solid book that develops all the basics of stat mech, and then covers some topics in chemical physics. Well-written, but dry. It's also fairly old, so the notation feels weird if you're used to modern books.

Sethna - Statistical Mechanics: Entropy, Order Parameters, and Complexity

Landau & Lifshitz - Statistical Physics, Part 1
